"Turtle hatcheries"

    In Sri lanka

 

"Of the seven living sea turtle species recorded in the world, five species were reported in the coastal belt of Sri Lanka coming for nesting: Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas),Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea), Hawksbill (Eretmochelys imbricata), Loggerhead (Caretta caretta) and Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea). The south and southwest coastline of Sri Lanka comprises the largest marine turtle rookeries (Amarasooriya, 2000). All five species are still recorded at Kosgoda,Induruwa, Rekawa and Bundala. Little bit from history

  • In Sri Lanka, there has been a long history of turtle hatcheries. The Sri Lankan government declared five species of sea turtles to be protected under the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance in the early 1970s.

  • The widespread hunting of sea turtles for their meat, shells, and eggs prompted this. Nevertheless, despite the legal protection, poaching and habitat loss led to a sustained drop in turtle populations. To help safeguard and conserve these threatened species, a number of private individuals and conservation groups erected turtle hatcheries along the nation's southern coast in the late 1970s.

  • These hatcheries gathered the eggs that were left on the beaches and incubated them until they hatched. Turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka have developed over time to become significant hubs for sea turtle education and protection.

  • The country's coastline now has more than 20 turtle hatcheries, with some of the more well-known ones being in Kosgoda, Bentota, and Habaraduwa.These hatcheries not only gather and incubate turtle eggs but also treat sick and injured turtles, inform tourists about the value of sea turtle conservation, and carry out biological and behavioral research on turtles.

  • Despite significant criticism over the years for specific procedures, turtle hatcheries are still a crucial part of Sri Lanka's attempts to save its sea turtle species.
